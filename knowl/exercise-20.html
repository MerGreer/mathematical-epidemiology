Exercise 5.

We make one more update to the pertussis model, addressing vaccine efficacy. Our inclusion of the multiplier \((1-p)\) assumed that vaccination had \(100 \%\) efficacy or effectiveness. However, research into pertussis vaccines shows that the level of vaccine efficacy or effectiveness can be significantly less. There is a wide range of values, depending on the specific vaccine used, the location in the world, and the years of study. For this exercise, we use a range of \(80-85\%\) efficacy. 9 
  1. Rewrite your model as below, where \(p\) remains the percentage of people vaccinated, and \(q\) is the efficacy percentage. We require that \(0 \leq p \leq 1\) and \(0 \leq q \leq 1\text{.}\) Explain in words why the way the model is written, and the ranges for \(p\) and \(q\text{,}\) make sense. In your explanation, include “edge cases”, that is, describe what happens when \(p=0\text{,}\) \(p=1\text{,}\) \(q=0\text{,}\) and \(q=1\text{.}\) Also describe what happens as \(p\) increases from \(0\) to \(1\text{,}\) and what happens as \(q\) increases from \(0\) to \(1\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} \frac{dS}{dt} \amp = - (1-p)(1-q) \beta S I \\ \frac{dE}{dt} \amp = (1-p)(1-q) \beta S I - \kappa E \\ \frac{dI}{dt} \amp = \kappa E - \gamma I \\ \frac{dR}{dt} \amp = \gamma I. \end{align*}

Use logic, as well as results from your code, to explain how vaccine efficacy below \(100\%\) can make it more challenging to achieve the goal of \(\mathcal{R}_{\text{eff}} \lt 1\text{.}\) In your explanation, you may wish to use the information that (6.1) used the assumption that vaccine was \(100\%\) effective.
